MoneyLion Assessment

Objective

  • To develop a ranking model that estimates and prioritizes users by their likelihood of early loan abandonment at application time, enabling more effective targeting, pricing, and operational decision-making.

Problem

  • This problem is framed as a ranking task rather than a binary classification task. At application time, the business does not need a hard yes/no prediction for each loan, but rather a prioritized list of applicants ordered by their likelihood of early abandonment. This ranking enables the business to allocate limited underwriting, marketing, and operational resources to applicants with the highest expected conversion.

def assign_withdraw_reason(row):
    if not row['has_clarity']:
        return 'Early abandonment'
    if row['fraud_flag_count'] >= 2:
        return 'Fraud indicators'
    if row['weak_identity_match']:
        return 'Identity mismatch'
    if row['high_velocity']:
        return 'High inquiry velocity'
    if row['thin_file_flag']:
        return 'Thin credit file'
    return 'Unknown'

Model Development

in real world scenario, it is very hard to figure out or classify if the applicant will early abandon or will continue the loan process until funded. what i plan to build is a ranker where it ranks the probability of those that least likely to abandon their application and focus on them rather than classify those who will get funded or those who witdraw from their application. then we will focus on them like maybe we can improve their User Experience or whatever that help them hooked to take loans with the bank.

'https://www.techtarget.com/searchenterpriseai/feature/Interpretability-vs-explainability-in-AI-and-machine-learning' thats why in this scenario i will just introduce logistic regression for this use case. as it is traditional ML and it lesser complex than other algorithm such as Neaural Network etc.

df_lift = pd.DataFrame({
    'y_true': y_val,
    'score': y_prob
})
df_lift = df_lift.sort_values('score').reset_index(drop=True)
baseline_rate = (df_lift['y_true'] == 0).mean()
percentiles = np.linspace(0.01, 0.5, 50)  # 1% to 50%
lifts = []

for p in percentiles:
    k = int(p * len(df_lift))
    top_k = df_lift.iloc[:k]
    rate = (top_k['y_true'] == 0).mean()
    lift = rate / baseline_rate
    lifts.append(lift)
plt.figure(figsize=(7, 5))
plt.plot(percentiles * 100, lifts, label='Model Lift')
plt.axhline(1, linestyle='--', label='Random Selection')

plt.xlabel('Top % of Users (Least Likely to Abandon)')
plt.ylabel('Lift (Non-Abandonment Rate / Baseline)')
plt.title('Lift Curve  Non-Abandonment Ranking')
plt.legend()
plt.grid()
plt.show()

The lift curve shows how much more likely a user is to fund compared to random selection as we move down the ranking. In the top 5% least-likely-to-abandon users, the model achieves over 6 lift, meaning these users are more than six times as likely to fund as an average applicant.

the baseline meaning, if we pick at random 100, only ~9 out of 100 that will not abandon. now if we focus on customers that the model thinks are least likely to abandon, how much better do we do than random? Compared to a baseline non-abandonment rate of 8.7%, the model increases non-abandonment to 37% in the top 10% least-likely users and over 54% in the top 5%, representing a 46 lift over random selection. This then demonstrated that the model is highly effective for priotization and resource allocation, even with highly imbalanced funnel.
